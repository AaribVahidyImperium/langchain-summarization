The history of artificial intelligence is a narrative of ambition, winter, and rebirth, stretching back over 70 years. While the dream of intelligent machines has ancient roots, the foundational work began in the 1940s and 1950s. In 1950, British mathematician Alan Turing published his seminal paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing the famous Turing Test as a measure of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from a human . This era also saw the creation of the first artificial neural networks, inspired by the human brain .

The official birth of AI as a field is universally marked by the **Dartmouth Summer Research Project in 1956** . Organized by John McCarthy, who coined the term "artificial intelligence," the workshop brought together leading thinkers like Marvin Minsky and Claude Shannon to explore the possibility of creating machines that could simulate every aspect of learning and intelligence . This optimism sparked initial breakthroughs, including the development of the **Logic Theorist**, considered the first AI program, and early work on perceptrons .

However, this golden age soon collided with the limitations of contemporary technology. Early AI systems, which relied on brute-force search and simple rules, could not handle complex, real-world problems. This led to the first "**AI winter**" in the 1970s, as government agencies like the U.S. and UK governments slashed funding due to a lack of practical results .

A resurgence came in the 1980s with the rise of "**expert systems**." These programs, such as the medical diagnosis tool MYCIN, codified the knowledge of human experts into rigid rules to perform specific tasks . This commercial viability attracted new investment . Yet, these systems were notoriously difficult to maintain and could not learn or adapt, eventually leading to a second, albeit milder, AI winter in the late 1980s and early 1990s .

The landscape began to shift in the late 1990s and 2000s, driven by two forces: **increased computational power and the explosion of digital data**. In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov, showcasing the power of brute-force computation on specialized problems . More importantly, a "bottom-up" approach inspired by neuroscience regained traction, focusing on neural networks that could learn from data .

This approach culminated in the modern era of **deep learning**. A pivotal moment was the 2012 ImageNet competition, where a neural network called **AlexNet** dramatically outperformed all rivals in image recognition . This breakthrough, fueled by the parallel processing power of GPUs and the vast datasets from the internet, unleashed a wave of progress . In 2016, **AlphaGo** defeated a world champion in the ancient game of Go, a feat once thought to be decades away .

Most recently, the development of large language models (LLMs) like **ChatGPT** (launched in 2022) has brought AI into the mainstream, demonstrating an unprecedented ability to understand and generate human-like text . Today, AI is no longer just a laboratory curiosity but a pervasive technology, with generative models creating art, code, and even scientific discoveries, reshaping the world as it continues its rapid evolution .
